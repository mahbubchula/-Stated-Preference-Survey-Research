## ğŸ¯ From Policy Questions to Research Objectives

### Step 1: Problem Framing

Start with a clear policy challenge. For example:

**Policy Challenge:** "Should our city implement dynamic pricing on express lanes?"

**Bad Research Objective:** "Understand traveler preferences for tolling"
- Too vague
- No measurable outcomes
- Unclear decision criteria

**Good Research Objectives:**
1. Estimate willingness-to-pay for 10-minute travel time savings during peak periods
2. Quantify demand elasticity across different income groups
3. Predict mode shift from general lanes to express lanes under three pricing scenarios
4. Identify acceptable price ranges that maintain 80% public support

### Step 2: Stakeholder Mapping

Identify who needs to be consulted and who will use the results:

| Stakeholder Group | Information Needs | Engagement Method |
|-------------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Decision-makers** | Cost-benefit ratios, political viability | Structured interviews |
| **Operations staff** | Implementation constraints, data availability | Technical workshops |
| **Community groups** | Equity impacts, access concerns | Focus groups |
| **Users (travelers)** | Real-world experiences, pain points | Cognitive interviews |
| **Advocacy organizations** | Environmental/social outcomes | Review panels |

### Step 3: Success Criteria Definition

Define what "good enough" looks like:

**Example Success Criteria:**
- Sample size: n â‰¥ 1,000 representative commuters
- Precision: Value of time estimated within Â±$2/hour (95% CI)
- Timeliness: Results delivered before Q3 budget cycle
- Budget: â‰¤ $75,000 total survey costs
- Actionability: Clear recommendation for 3 pricing scenarios

**Evaluation Criteria:**

- **Realism:** Can respondents evaluate it meaningfully?
- **Controllability:** Can it be manipulated in real implementation?
- **Measurability:** Can we observe changes in real-world outcomes?

### Common Attributes by Transport Context

#### Urban Transit Services
- **Frequency:** headway in minutes (5, 10, 15, 20, 30)
- **Fare:** absolute price or relative to current ($2.50, $3.00, $3.50, $4.00)
- **Travel time:** door-to-door minutes
- **Reliability:** percentage on-time performance (85%, 90%, 95%, 98%)
- **Access distance:** walk to stop (200m, 400m, 600m, 800m)
- **Comfort:** crowding level (seats available, standing room, crowded)

#### Road Pricing
- **Toll amount:** dynamic or fixed ($/trip)
- **Time savings:** minutes saved vs. free alternative
- **Reliability:** travel time variability (Â±2 min, Â±5 min, Â±10 min)
- **Revenue use:** description of how toll funds are spent
- **Exemptions:** who qualifies for discounts

#### Electric Vehicle Adoption
- **Purchase price:** relative to conventional vehicle (+$0, +$5k, +$10k, +$15k)
- **Range:** miles on single charge (150, 250, 350, 450)
- **Charging time:** fast charge duration (15, 30, 45, 60 minutes)
- **Fuel savings:** annual operating cost reduction
- **Charging infrastructure:** density of charging stations

### Level Definition Best Practices

**Realistic Ranges:**
- Base levels on actual market conditions
- Extend 20-30% beyond current bounds to capture future scenarios
- Avoid unrealistic combinations (e.g., $1 toll with zero time savings)

**Perceptual Thresholds:**
- Use differences respondents can actually perceive
- Time: 5-minute increments for short trips, 10-15 for longer
- Cost: 25-50 cent increments for low-value items
- Distance: 100-200 meter increments for walking

**Example: Transit Fare Study**

```
Current fare: $2.75

Proposed levels:
- Level 1: $2.25 (-18% baseline)
- Level 2: $2.50 (-9% baseline)
- Level 3: $2.75 (status quo)
- Level 4: $3.00 (+9% baseline)
- Level 5: $3.50 (+27% baseline)
```

**Rationale:**
- Symmetric around status quo for unbiased estimation
- 25-cent increments are perceptually meaningful
- Range captures plausible policy debate space
- Five levels allow non-linear preference detection

## ğŸ‘¥ Sampling Strategy & Target Populations

### Defining the Sampling Frame

**Questions to Answer:**
1. **Who is the population of interest?**
   - Current users only, or potential users too?
   - Geographic boundaries?
   - Demographic restrictions (e.g., licensed drivers, age 18+)?

2. **What is the required sample size?**
   - Minimum for statistical power (typically n â‰¥ 300-500)
   - Segmentation needs (n â‰¥ 100 per subgroup)
   - Budget constraints

3. **How will representativeness be ensured?**
   - Probability sampling vs. quota sampling
   - Weighting strategies for known biases
   - Over-sampling of rare populations

### Sample Size Determination

**Rule of Thumb for MNL Models:**

```
Minimum n = (# of parameters to estimate) Ã— 50

Example:
- 3 alternatives
- 4 attributes per alternative
- 2 alternative-specific constants
Total parameters â‰ˆ 14

Minimum sample: 14 Ã— 50 = 700 observations
If 8 choice tasks per respondent: 700 Ã· 8 â‰ˆ 88 respondents
Recommended: 200-300 respondents for buffer
```

**Advanced Models (Mixed Logit, Latent Class):**
- Multiply base calculation by 1.5 to 2.0
- Account for convergence requirements
- Plan for 20-30% attrition/incomplete surveys

### Quota Design Example

**Transit Redesign Study in Mid-Sized City**

| Segment | Population % | Target n | Recruitment Channel |
|---------|--------------|----------|---------------------|

- Urban core: 40% (n=400)

**Socioeconomic Quotas:**
- Income <$50k: 30%

## ğŸŒ Real-World Applications & Case Studies

### Case Study 1: London Congestion Charge Expansion

**Challenge:** Assess public acceptance of extending congestion charging zone

**Survey Design Approach:**
- **Sample:** 3,500 London residents + 1,500 commuters from surrounding areas
- **Key Attributes:**
  - Charge zone boundary (3 options)
  - Price level (Â£8, Â£10, Â£12, Â£15)
  - Operating hours (7am-7pm vs. 24/7)
  - Exemptions (residents, EVs, taxis)
  - Revenue use (transit, roads, general fund)

**Attribute Development Process:**
1. **Stakeholder workshops** with Transport for London, boroughs, business groups
2. **Focus groups** (8 sessions, n=72) to test attribute wording
3. **Cognitive interviews** (n=24) to validate scenario comprehension
4. **Pilot survey** (n=200) to refine levels

**Key Design Innovation:**
Used **pivot design** where charge levels were personalized relative to respondent's current travel cost, improving realism and engagement.

**Outcomes:**
- Identified Â£11 as optimal price point balancing revenue and public support
- Discovered strong preference for reinvesting revenue in transit improvements
- Informed 2023 expansion decision

### Case Study 2: Rural Demand-Responsive Transit

**Challenge:** Design on-demand shuttle service for low-density areas

**Survey Design Approach:**
- **Sample:** 800 residents in 3 pilot counties, stratified by:
  - Age (seniors over-sampled)
  - Car ownership
  - Current transit use

- **Key Attributes:**
  - Booking method (phone, app, both)
  - Maximum wait time (15, 30, 45, 60 min)
  - Fare ($2, $4, $6, $8 per trip)
  - Operating hours (peak only, extended, 24/7)
  - Shared ride probability (always private, 50% shared, always shared)

**Attribute Refinement:**
- **Initial plan:** Include vehicle type (van vs. sedan)
- **Focus group feedback:** Respondents didn't care about vehicle; cared about accessibility features
- **Final design:** Replaced "vehicle type" with "wheelchair accessible (yes/no)"

**Survey Flow Innovation:**
Included a **"Current Trip Diary"** before SP tasks:
- Respondents logged 3 recent trips that were difficult without a car
- SP scenarios were framed as alternatives for those specific trips
- Dramatically improved realism scores (from 3.2/5 in pilot to 4.5/5 in main)

**Results:**
- Wait time was 2.5x more important than price
- Strong segmentation by age: seniors valued phone booking much higher
- Service deemed viable if 30-minute max wait could be guaranteed

### Case Study 3: Airline Baggage Fee Unbundling

**Challenge:** Optimize pricing for checked bag, priority boarding, seat selection

**Survey Design Complexity:**
- **Attributes:**
  - Base fare
  - Checked bag fee (1st bag, 2nd bag)
  - Seat selection fee
  - Priority boarding fee
  - Loyalty points earned

**Problem:** Too many attributes (8 total) created cognitive overload

**Solution: Partial Profile Design**
- Each choice task showed only 4-5 attributes
- Attributes rotated across tasks
- More choice tasks (12) to maintain statistical power
- Allowed estimation despite complexity

**Outcome:**
- Successfully estimated willingness-to-pay for all service components
- Identified optimal bundling strategies
- Informed dynamic pricing algorithms

### Prompt 1: Cultural Context in Attribute Design

**Question:**
"How should cultural norms and local context influence attribute wording or the design of incentive structures? Provide a specific example from a region or community you're familiar with."

**Discussion Guidelines:**
- Consider language, literacy levels, technology access
- Think about social desirability bias (e.g., environmental attitudes)
- Address trust in government/institutions
- Discuss incentive preferences (cash, vouchers, charity donations)

**Example Starter:**
"In rural areas with limited internet access, phone-based surveys may be preferred, but this constrains the use of visual aids..."

### Prompt 2: Survey Length vs. Depth Trade-off

**Question:**
"What trade-offs exist between survey length and the richness of attitudinal measures? When is it worth adding 5 minutes to capture psychographic data?"

**Consider:**
- Impact on completion rates
- Value of auxiliary information (attitudes, perceptions, lifestyles)
- Sample representativeness vs. sample size
- Budget implications

**Challenge:**
"Defend a position: Either 'Keep it short and simple' OR 'Invest in comprehensive profiling.'"

### Prompt 3: Attribute Overload Dilemma

**Question:**
"You've identified 10 attributes that stakeholders insist are 'all critical.' How do you reduce this to a manageable 5-6 without alienating decision-makers?"

**Approach Strategies:**
- Quantitative prioritization methods
- Phased study design (Study 1 vs. Study 2)
- Partial profile designs
- Post-estimation scenario analysis to address "missing" attributes

**Share:**
A real or hypothetical negotiation process you would use.
